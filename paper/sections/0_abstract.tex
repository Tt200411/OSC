Activation functions inside long-sequence forecasting transformers are usually inherited as implementation defaults, even though they control saturation, sign preservation, and gradient scale inside the feed-forward blocks. We test whether this default is harmless by running an activation-only Informer grid over ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2, and eight Solar sites: 36 dataset-horizon cells, nine activation configurations, and three seeds per configuration, for 972 matched seed-runs. The architecture, attention type, width, depth, optimizer, data splits, batch size, and epoch budget are held fixed inside each cell. Non-GELU activations are best in 34 of 36 cells; only Solar3 at horizons 24 and 96 remains GELU-best. Bounded and tanh-family activations are the strongest static choices: \texttt{tanh\_sin001}, \texttt{tanh}, and \texttt{softsign} have the best mean ranks and jointly account for 23 best cells. The same grid also rejects a universal replacement rule. Output \texttt{tanh} wins several high-gain ETT/ETTm cells but is poor on average, ReLU wins only one cell, and factor-oracle diagnostics provide weak leave-one-seed-out evidence for regime routing. PatchTST and iTransformer stress tests further show that the Informer ranking is not architecture-invariant. The supported conclusion is narrow but useful: under a matched Informer protocol, activation choice is a dataset- and horizon-sensitive design variable, not a harmless default.
